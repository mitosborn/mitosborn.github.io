Rice University

Data Science Researcher

Houston, TX May 2020 - April 2021

As a researcher in Dr. Sylvia Dee's Climate Lab, I investigated the environmental impacts of the COVID-19 lockdown on Texas. To do this, I utilized statistical modeling on historical data to predict what environmental indicator levels would have been if the lockdown didn't occur in each of Texas' 254 counties. I then divided the data into three partitions (pre-lockdown, lockdown, and post-lockdown) and performed significance testing. I found that the reductions in pollutants from the lockdown were mostly cancelled out by the effects of reopening.

To best communicate these results, I built a web dashboard utilizing the Python package Dash. This dashboard utilizes an interactive heat-map, time series plot, and scatter plot to visualize trends of eight environmental and two socioeconomic indicators throughout the lockdown.

To complete this work, I worked closely with a diverse team of environmental science, computer science, and statistics majors. The results of this research and the subsequent dashboard were presented at the American Geophysical Union conference. While the paper is in the process of being published.

COVID-19 Environmental Impacts Dashboard

Rice Climate Laboratory

Spring 2020 - April 2021 | Dashboard | Github

COVID-19 Environmental Impacts Dashboard
Previous Next

Description: Year long project to assess environmental and socioeconomic impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic on Texas. I worked in a team of 8 students of various majors to design and implement a data science pipeline to assess COVID's impacts. This pipeline collected the most recent environmental and socioeconomic county level data, compared historical and present data using statistics and time-series modelling, and visualized impacts by metropolitan area and county with a dashboard.

To implement this pipeline, I first utilized Selenium to automate downloading historical data from the Texas Comission for Environmental Quality (TCEQ) due to their lack of an API. Then, I leveraged Python's Pandas and Statsmodels packages to group data by county and metropolitan area and model what indicator levels would have been if the pandemic never occurred. Finally, to improve interpretability and simplify analysis of the data, I created an interactive dashboard using Dash, a Python dashboarding package.

The results of this research and the subsequent dashboard were presented at the American Geophysical Union conference. Furthermore, the paper is in the process of being published.

Packages used: Dash, Plotly, Matplotlib, Statsmodels, Pandas, Selenium.

PageRank

COMP 330: Tools and Models for Data Science

Fall 2021 | Dashboard | Github

PageRank
Previous Next

Description: In this project, the goal was to implement PageRank, an algorithm well-known as the basis of Google's original search engine. The algorithm functions on a directed graph of web pages, where an edge exists between webpages A and B if there exists a link to page B on page A. On execution, the algortihm simulates a web surfer who randomly selects a link on the current page to navigate to next. The PageRank is the probability that when the web surfer stops clicking, the user will be on a specific page.

I implemented this algorithm using SQL. As this algorithm is iterative and SQL is a language that performs best with declarative code, I did as much calculation as possible declaratively. This project was critical in improving my SQL skills and my understanding of data operations I often perform in Pandas that originated in SQL (Ex: Join).

Predicting Flight Delays

COMP 340: Statistical Models for Data Science

Spring 2020 - April 2021 | Dashboard | Github

Predicting Flight Delays
Previous Next

Description: In this assignment, I had to predict whether a flight would experience a delay of greater than an hour based predictor variables such as the weather, distance traveled, and departure time. Utilizing sklearn, I built three models to do this task:

  • Logistic regression with an L2 penalty
  • Logistic regression with an L1 penalty
  • Naive Bayes (GaussianNB)


The data was structured such that each row represented a flight with distance, departure + destination airports, total delay (in minutes), and weather information (this was merged from another dataset). I converted the delay column into a binary vector where 1 represented a delayed flight by 60 or more minutes, and 0 the flight was delayed by less than that threshold. This binary delay column became the y vector I was trying to predict and the rest of the data became the matrix of feature vectors, X.

After training the three models outlined above on this data, I found that the two logistic regression models performed identically at predicting delays of > 60 minutes. While Gaussian Naive Bayes model performed the worst. Looking at the feature weights of the logistic models, the L1 penalty logistic model used precipitation as the greatest indicator of delay, and the L2 penalty logistic model decided temperature best indicated a delay.

Packages used: Pandas, Scikit-learn, and Numpy.
